Territorial United States

An interactive model of the territory now governed by the United States, from 1750 — when it was Native ground contested by three empires — through the present day. Scrub the timeline to watch sovereignty claims, borders, cities, infrastructure and ecology change; click any state, territory, city, river, park or overlay for a card that rewrites itself for the year you are viewing.

METHOD & GEOMETRY

Modern state and coastline geometry comes from the U.S. Census Bureau cartographic boundary files (via us-atlas); rivers and lakes from Natural Earth. Acquisition boundaries are hand-traced for this visualization along their treaty definitions — the Mississippi, Sabine, Red, Arkansas, Rio Grande and Gila rivers, the 31st, 42nd and 49th parallels, the 100th meridian and the Adams-Onís line — and are simplified: they are faithful at continental scale, not surveyor-accurate. Area figures follow the Census Bureau's 1912 acquisition accounting; the modern total (~3.80 million sq mi) reflects later remeasurement.

HONESTY OF THE MAP

Every acquisition shown here transferred land between empires on paper; on the ground it was the homeland of sovereign Native nations, taken by treaty, coercion and war. Their boundaries shifted continuously and are under-represented by any polygon — this model carries that history in its cards, chapters and events rather than pretending a single map could hold it.

SIMPLIFICATIONS

  • Territorial organization (e.g. the many shapes of Missouri or Dakota Territory) is summarized in cards, not drawn year-by-year.
  • Colonial-era claims are stylized; the Maine and Oregon boundary disputes resolve at their treaty dates (1842, 1846).
  • Overseas territories — the Philippines (1898–1946), Panama Canal Zone (1903–79), Puerto Rico, Guam, American Samoa, the U.S. Virgin Islands, the Northern Marianas and Hawaiʻi — sit at their true locations on the world frame; the story camera flies to each as it joins, and the buttons beside the acquisitions legend fly there anytime.
  • The landscape itself is simulated through time. The satellite base (NASA Blue Marble, July 2004) is joined by four computed historical variants that crossfade along the timeline: the primeval eastern forest (1750), the clearing frontier and peak New England farmland (1850), maximum deforestation with the Great Lakes cutover and half-plowed prairie (1900), and mid-century recovery (1950). Reservoirs appear only after their dams; the Salton Sea after 1905; snowpack runs slightly larger in early centuries; irrigated western valleys fade to dryland before their canals. These are directional reconstructions built from the historical record — impressionistic, not survey data.
  • The human world grows too: urban footprints for two dozen metros expand along Census urbanized-area figures (watch Los Angeles fill its basin after 1900), the trunk railroads web the map from the 1850s, and the Interstate system spreads after 1956.
  • City populations are city-proper census figures; national population interpolates between censuses.

ONE WORLD MAP, TRADE & TECHNOLOGY

The map is a single, continuous satellite Earth (US-centered Equal Earth projection): zoom out or press the World map button and the whole planet is there, with overseas territories at their true locations, and the Trade & treaties layer drawing the republic's great flows — tobacco, the Atlantic slave trade, cotton, kerosene, Lend-Lease, the China trade — and its treaties and alliances. The Technology and Infrastructure & energy layers place invention and industry on the ground where they happened, from the cotton gin to Bell Labs to the AI data-center boom; the readout tracks GDP, electricity, oil, rail, steel, military strength and American compute across time (approximate series — Census Bureau, BEA, EIA, AISI, DoD, industry estimates).

PRIMARY SOURCES

National Archives milestone documents (Treaty of Paris 1783, Louisiana Purchase, Guadalupe Hidalgo, Alaska purchase check, Hawaiʻi annexation resolution); Avalon Project treaty texts (Yale Law School); U.S. Census Bureau decennial censuses and Area of the United States (1912); Library of Congress; National Park Service; U.S. Geological Survey; state archives and historical societies as cited on each card.
